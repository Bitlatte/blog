## What It's Really Like to Code with AI

### I. The "Why": Turning to AI for a Practical Coding Project

You know how it is. You get an idea for a project – for me, it was building my own static site generator (SSG). I’d looked around, and nothing quite fit. NextJS for a simple static site? Seemed like bringing a cannon to a knife fight. Hugo? Super powerful, sure, but it felt like I'd spend more time figuring out *its* features than actually writing my blog. I just wanted something dead simple that would let me write content without a fuss.

The big catch, though? Time. Or, more like, no time. I’ve only got a couple of hours a week, tops, to tinker with code. Building an SSG from the ground up, even a basic one, felt like a pipe dream with that kind of schedule.

Now, I’ve been using AI for coding bits and pieces for a while now – it's not like this was my first rodeo. But recently, I had a bit of a lightbulb moment. What if I changed my strategy? What if I really leaned on it, let AI do the *heavy lifting* for this SSG project? I’d heard good things about tools like Gemini, and was starting to play with Google Jules. Could they actually help me get a significant project off the ground, given my super-limited coding time?

So, I decided to give this new approach a serious shot. My goal wasn't just to dabble; I wanted to see if AI could genuinely become a core part of my development workflow, especially for the more time-consuming tasks. Here's an honest look at how using AI for the heavy lifting in my coding process has actually played out.

### II. My First Steps: Learning to "Talk" to an AI Coding Assistant

So, I decided to really lean on AI for this SSG project. Even though I'd used AI for little coding tasks here and there before, this felt different. I was essentially asking it to be a core developer, not just a syntax checker or a snippet generator. Honestly, I wasn’t entirely sure what to expect. Would it get what I was trying to do? Would it be more work wrestling with the AI than just coding it myself, even with my limited time?

One of the first big tests was when I wanted to add support for HTML "partials" – you know, those reusable chunks of code like headers or footers that you want on multiple pages. Instead of just trying to bash out the logic myself, I turned to Gemini. I remember telling it something like, "Help me make my templating system more robust by allowing me to create partials, or small chunks of code that get reused in multiple places."

And bam! The code it spat out was pretty solid. I gave it a once-over, and it was all there, surprisingly in-depth and, get this, it worked pretty much on the first try. No endless back-and-forth, no weird bugs to squash right out of the gate. That was a real "aha!" moment for me. Okay, I thought, this might actually work.

That success with the partials wasn't just a fluke; it was a direct result of how I was learning to communicate with the AI. At first, I was probably a bit too simplistic, like just barking out "I want HTML partials!" But I quickly learned that the more I treated it like a conversation, the better the results. I started writing longer, more thought-out prompts, really describing the *why* and the *how* of what I needed. It felt less like giving orders and more like brainstorming with a (very logical) partner.

Another thing that clicked was the importance of keeping the AI in the loop. Since Gemini was involved from early on, it had a good grasp of the existing codebase. If I tweaked something myself, I'd make sure to tell it about the changes. This way, the new code it generated usually slotted right in without much fuss. It was like building up a shared understanding of the project.

### III. AI in Action: The Ups and Downs of AI-Driven Feature Development

With a better handle on how to "talk" to the AI, things really started moving. And the biggest, most obvious win? Speed. I was spending drastically less time spinning up new features than I ever did coding solo. Ideas I’d jot down during the week could actually get turned into code when I found a spare hour or two, simply by tasking Gemini or Jules with them. That pressure to finish something in one sitting because I might forget where I was? Gone. I could stop midway through a complex feature, and the AI would usually pick right back up where we left off. It was a massive productivity boost.

But it wasn't all smooth sailing, especially when things got complicated. For instance, taking my project from a basic Markdown-to-HTML converter to a full-fledged static site generator – that was a struggle. There were times I wasn't even sure exactly what I was trying to ask the AI, which, as you can imagine, made it tough for the AI to deliver what I (vaguely) wanted. It really highlighted that the AI, for all its power, isn't a mind reader. Clarity on my end was crucial.

To handle those moments when we'd go off on a tangent or the AI's suggestions got a bit wild, I developed this quirky little system. I started telling it to "save" our progress at certain points, like a save spot in a video game. If things went sideways, I could just say, "Hey, let's go back to that last save," and we could rewind to a point where things made sense. It sounds a bit odd, but it was a lifesaver for managing those more complex iterations.

One of the pleasant surprises, though, was documentation. Man, what a breeze! The AI did a fantastic job whipping up code comments and even generating Markdown docs for features. The Markdown sometimes needed a bit of formatting help for copy-pasting, but honestly, it was a tiny price to pay for having most of the tedious documentation work done automatically.

And yeah, the AI would sometimes get ambitious, suggesting more advanced features or different ways to build something. While some of those ideas were interesting, I always made sure *I* was the one making the final calls, especially to keep the SSG aligned with my original goal of simplicity. It was good to know it could offer alternatives, but I was definitely still in the driver's seat.

### IV. The Nature of AI-Generated Code & Its Impact on Me

So, what about the actual code the AI was churning out? If I'm being totally honest, the quality often surprised me – in a good way. The code was generally more robust than what I’d typically write on my own, especially when I was rushing. It was better about including things like tests, and the commenting was way more consistent. I actually feel pretty confident that if I look back at this project years from now, I’ll be able to understand what’s going on, which definitely wasn't always the case with my old solo projects after a few weeks away from them!

And it wasn't just about the final code; I learned stuff too. The AI would sometimes suggest a new concept or a different way of approaching a problem that I wasn't familiar with. I’d then ask it to explain, and boom – I’d pick up something new. That was a nice, unexpected perk.

But here’s where it gets a bit complicated for me, emotionally. Even though the ideas for the SSG were mine, and I was guiding the AI, I often felt weirdly disconnected from the codebase. It was almost like it didn't truly belong to me. I’m used to spending hours, sometimes days, wrestling with a complex problem, taking breaks, learning something new, and then finally having that breakthrough moment. That struggle, and the eventual triumph, makes you feel like you’ve *earned* the result.

With AI, I could hit a wall, and instead of that deep dive, I could just say, "Hey, this isn't working, let's figure out why and find another way." And often, we would, quickly. But that speed, that ease, sometimes left me feeling like I hadn't really earned it. It’s a strange feeling, almost like I'd cheated my way to a passing grade in school. I even felt a bit ashamed about it, and for a while, I didn't want to talk or write about this project because I felt like a bit of a fraud.

At the end of the day, though, I have to be realistic. I've got so much on my plate, and my free time is incredibly limited. So, while the "earned it" feeling might be a bit diminished, the fact is, I have a working, evolving project. And to me, some code, even AI-assisted code, is a whole lot better than no code at all.

### V. Broader Reflections on Coding with an AI Partner

Using AI this intensively for a real project has definitely got me thinking about the bigger picture. For starters, there's the whole skill development angle. On one hand, like I said, my understanding of certain concepts has genuinely improved because the AI would bring up new stuff, and I’d learn by asking it to explain. That’s a clear win.

But on the other hand, I can’t shake the feeling that my own direct coding "muscles" might be getting a bit soft. Is that because I'm relying on AI for the heavy lifting, or just because my actual hands-on keyboard time for coding is so scarce these days? It's probably a bit of both, and it’s something I’m still mulling over. It's a weird balance – learning more conceptually while potentially doing less of the direct, nitty-gritty implementation myself.

Looking ahead, I don’t think programmers are going anywhere anytime soon. This experience hasn't convinced me that AI is about to take all our jobs. But it *has* convinced me that AI absolutely has a serious place in the programming world. For a lot of tasks, especially if you're not building something wildly creative or hyper-complex, the AI – if you guide it carefully – is probably more capable of churning out solid first drafts of code than I am, especially when I'm short on time or mental energy.

As for the "ethical" side of things, for a personal project like this, my main struggle was that internal "fraud" feeling I mentioned earlier. I wasn't too worried about things like code ownership or job displacement in this context. But the reliance on the tool, and that question of skill atrophy, those are definitely things that sit in the back of my mind. It’s a new kind of dependency, and I think we’re all still figuring out what that means.

### VI. Should *You* Use AI to Code? My Advice and Final Thoughts

So, after all this, the big question: should *you* jump in and start using AI for your coding projects? My honest answer is... maybe! It’s not going to be for everyone, and it's certainly not a magic wand. You might try it and absolutely hate the feeling, or you might find it clicks for you like it eventually did for me. The only real way to know is to give it a go and see how it feels for your own workflow and your own projects.

As for me, I'm definitely sticking with it. My time constraints aren't magically disappearing – if anything, they're likely to get tighter. So, I see myself relying on AI even more in the future. At this point, I can’t really imagine tackling any significant personal coding project without AI playing some kind of role, whether it's for heavy lifting, brainstorming, or just getting through the more tedious bits.

Looking back, I started this SSG project wanting a simple tool, but I was constantly up against the clock. AI, despite its quirks and the weird emotional baggage it sometimes brought along, became the key to actually making progress. It allowed me to translate ideas into working code in the slivers of free time I had. It wasn't always a perfect process, and it definitely reshaped my understanding of what "building" something means. But ultimately, it helped me bring a personal project to life that otherwise would probably still be just a half-baked idea in a notebook.

And that, for me, makes exploring this new way of coding totally worth it.
